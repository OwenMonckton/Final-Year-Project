How does a rocket engine create the forces needed for thrust?

As talked about here, rocket engines generate thrust by accelerating the exhaust gasses out which creates the force to generate thrust. Now I want to talk about how these engines are able to accelerate the gasses.

Why engines look the way they do

Most engines tend to have this hour glass looking shape, this is to exploit Bernoulli's principle. Bernoulli's equations are long and complex but what they mean is such: when you have a fluid travelling down a tube it will have a set flow rate. This flow rate is a combination of how much water is moving down the tube and how fast it is moving. When the pressure is consistent, this flow rate is consistent. So no matter how you change the shape of the tube it will always have the same flow of a fluid through it.

What this means in the case of rocket engines is that if you pinch a section of the tube, restricting the amount of fluid that can flow through, the fluid must speed up to have the same flow rate as before. This acceleration of the fluid is what generates the thrust by producing a force.
